    What is cancer ?

    Cancer is a group of diseases characterised by abnormal cells that divide uncontrollably, infiltrating and destroying healthy tissue in the process. Cancer often has the ability to spread throughout the body. It is the second leading cause of death worldwide. However, survival rates are improving for many types of cancer thanks to advances in screening, treatment and prevention.

    What is a relative risk (RR) ?

    Relative risk (RR) is a measure that compares the likelihood of experiencing a health-related event (such as illness, injury, exposure to a risk factor, or death) in one group to the likelihood of experiencing the same event in another group. It does this by dividing the risk (incidence proportion, attack rate) in group 1 by the risk (incidence proportion, attack rate) in group 2. The two groups are usually differentiated by demographic factors such as gender (e.g. men versus women) or exposure to a presumed risk factor. The group of most interest is often called the 'exposed group', and the comparison group is called the 'unexposed group'. This website always compares the risk for a smoker to that for a non-smoker.


    In this case, the relative risk analysis compares the probability of a smoker developing cancer with that of a non-smoker. the relative risk is 1, then smokers and non-smokers have the same risk of developing cancer. If the RR is less than 1, then smokers are at a lower risk. Conversely, if the RR is greater than 1, then smokers are at a greater risk of developing cancer than non-smokers. For example, a relative risk of 9 for lung cancer means that a smoker is 9 times more likely to develop lung cancer than a non-smoker.
